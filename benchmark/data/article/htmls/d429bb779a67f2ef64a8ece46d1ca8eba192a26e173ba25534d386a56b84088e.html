The Eurovision Song Contest is normally a homage to glitter, glamour and high camp. This year it was very much about the toxic politics of the Middle East.

A howling mob gathered outside the venue in Sweden, chanted anti-Semitic slogans and demanded Israel's expulsion from the competition over the war in Gaza.

The venom was so intense that Eden Golan, the 20-year-old representing Israel, was confined to her hotel under police protection until she was due to perform.

As she took to the stage, there were boos from some in the audience. In the UK, pro-Palestine groups intimidated some pubs and bars into cancelling screenings.

How shameful that these people stood with Hamas, rather than the Jews murdered, raped and kidnapped by the terror group at another music festival on October 7.

Echoing the mood, the professional judges awarded Israel a paltry 52 points, putting them well down the leaderboard.

Then something remarkable happened. In the public vote, Israel was the overwhelming choice. An extraordinary 323 points – including a maximum 12 from the UK – propelled Ms Golan in to fifth place.

Eurovision is hardly a scientific poll but it does give an intriguing snapshot of public opinion. It's clear the protesters running amok in London and our universities do not represent the silent majority.

Unlike the sanctimonious demonstrators and the woke establishment that panders to them, ordinary people believe Israel is right to defend itself against a deadly enemy. Nor do they like anti-Semitism, bullying or the glorification of terrorism.

That's why people voted in their droves for Ms Golan (who is a good singer, too).

It would be gratifying if certain politicians took note. They should stop equivocating over their support for Israel – and start taking firmer action against Hamas apologists staining our streets with racism.

PM's grim warning

Rishi Sunak's assessment that the next few years will be 'some of the most dangerous' for Britain could not be more worrying.

Rapid advances in technology, mass migration and muscle-flexing by authoritarian states such as China will have a seismic impact on our democracy, economy and society, warns the PM.

Yes, seizing the opportunities offered by, for instance, AI, the UK can build a more prosperous and secure future. But it is reassuring that Mr Sunak feels a 'profound sense of urgency' to tackle the threats.

In that case, shouldn't he aim to spend 3 per cent of GDP on defence by 2030, rather than the 2.5 pledged? And he must slash unsustainable immigration levels that are harming public services and social cohesion.

Mr Sunak insists he has 'bold ideas', which is more than can be said for Sir Keir Starmer. With the election looming, it's imperative he begins putting them into effect.

A weighty problem

We knew that the obesity epidemic was a drag anchor on the economy. Today, a landmark study reveals just how badly.

Up to 60 per cent of Britons may be on sick leave due to weight-related conditions. This is costing an estimated £98billion a year in lost productivity and taxes, NHS treatments and benefits.

But action is needed. The obesity crisis has weighed Britain down for too long.
